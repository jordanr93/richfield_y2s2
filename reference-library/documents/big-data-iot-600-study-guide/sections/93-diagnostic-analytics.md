   Diagnostic Analytics

   Diagnostic analytics digs deeper into the data to understand why something happened. It
   goes beyond just summarizing trends and looks for the root causes of specific outcomes. For
   instance, if sales dropped last quarter, diagnostic analytics would help uncover factors like
   market changes, customer complaints, or operational inefficiencies that might have
   contributed to the decline. It’s all about identifying why things happened.

   Predictive Analytics

   Predictive analytics takes things a step further by using data, statistical algorithms, and
   machine learning techniques to predict future events. This type of analysis is about
   forecasting trends and behaviors based on past data. For example, a retailer might use
   predictive analytics to forecast demand for a product during the holiday season. By looking
   at historical trends, they can predict what customers are likely to buy next. In essence, it’s
   about answering the question: What could happen?

   Prescriptive Analytics

   Prescriptive analytics goes beyond prediction by recommending actions you should take. It
   combines data analysis with decision-making models to help you choose the best course of
   action for a given situation. For example, prescriptive analytics might help a logistics company
   optimize delivery routes based on traffic data, weather patterns, and historical performance.
   This type of analytics is focused on answering: What should we do about it?

   In summary, each type of data analytics serves a different purpose—descriptive tells you
   what happened, diagnostic explains why it happened, predictive forecasts future outcomes,
